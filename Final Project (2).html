Introduction and Motivation:

Our goal is to examine how citizenship, gender, occupation, state, and family composition influence working hours, in order to enhance our understanding of participants' labor market behavior. In particular, we are interested in examining how these variables affect the number of hours worked and if specific populations are more inclined to longer or shorter working hours. This issue is set within the global context of contemporary labor markets characterized by the interplay of economic, sociocultural, and political factors that dictate the work life balance. Dismissing the importance of work hours is economically counterproductive, socially conservative, and regressive. In addition, work hours differ among certain demographic groups due to the availability of employment, family responsibilities, social attitudes toward gender, and immigration rules. Through this analysis, we strive to offer insights for formulating policy geared towards creating discrimination-free laws in the labor market, fostering inclusive workplaces, and making family and career choices. Advances in understanding work participation disparities, workforce policies, and economic equity will require grappling with these dynamics. To substantiate this inquiry are studies of the labor market, employment indicators, and economic theories of social inequality and work-life balance, which help contextualize and validate the findings.

Literature Review

Children and Careers: How Family Size Affects Parents Labor Market Outcomes in the Long Run

  • Author: Sara Cools, Simen Markussen, Marte Strom
  • Link to article

The correlation between family size and the workforce, mainly weekly hours worked, has received considerable focus. Cools et al. (2017) use instrumental variables to analyze this and demonstrate that family size strongly and negatively affects mothers weekly working hours while fathers hours remain primarily unchanged. In essence, they argue that each additional child decreases mothers labor supply by, on average, 3-5 hours a week, with more pronounced effects during the early years following childbirth and enduring impacts in the scenario of highly educated women. As described, the study illustrates Beckers well-known economic concepts, a posteriori validation of the econometric assumptions of the Beckerian household model, and a quantity-quality trade-off, which states that an increase in market work necessitated in response to increased childcare duties. Additionally, robust gaps in gender differences in the labor supply perpetuate the maternal employment paradigm, which positions women as principal caregivers. These findings suggest an urgency for more attention to subsidized child care, flexible work hours, active father involvement, and a reduction in the detrimental effects on womens work hours. The study illustrates the implications of family size on parental participation in the labor market alongside the need for policies on gender inequality in the labor market.

Gender Inequality, Work Hours, and the Future of Work

  • Authors: Ariane Hegewisch, M.Phil., and Valerie Lacarte, Ph.D.
  • Link to report

The Institute for Womens Policy Research in 2020 stipulated that there is an unequal allocation of work opportunities to men and women, which in one way or another influences the number of hours each gender is required to work. Women, in most cases, do not work as many hours as men in a week because of an amalgamation of caregiving duties, social norms, and a lack of accepted domestic help, which women have to shoulder alongside their primary work. Women are more likely than men to work shorter hours, not because they want to, but because they are trying to juggle caregiving responsibilities, which decreases their earnings. But it seems men actively take on more full-time and overtime positions that offer set hours. Thus, their earnings increase substantially compared to women's because established practices confirm that subordinate social classes have to work hard to earn good money, and this is especially true in low-paying jobs within the service sector where women are hopelessly trapped.

Immigration and work schedules: Theory and evidence

  • Authors: Timothy N. Bond, Osea Giuntella, Jakub Lonsky
  • Link to report

Regardless of how their weekly employment compares to that of native workers, immigrants are known to work more hours per week, especially during the night. In a study conducted by Bond et al. (2023), it was established that immigrants disproportionately fill positions featuring longer or irregular hours because of economic necessity, lower disutility costs, and lack of alternative employment options. Consequently, native workers in regions with higher inflows of immigrant workers tend to receive disproportionate daytime shifts. The reasoning is that immigration to an area causes a decline in compensating differentials for night work. Immigrants tend to accept lower wages and work night shifts, which are undesirable. The study shows that a 10 percentage point increase in the share of immigrants in the workforce decreases the probability of natives working night shifts by 0.8 percentage points and increases wages for daytime shifts. The findings highlight that citizenship status is crucial in allocating work schedules, especially non-citizens, who are overrepresented and fill lower-amenity, extended, or irregular-hours jobs. Even as natives enjoy flexible access to standard work schedules, immigrants are compelled to work longer hours in low-paying, labor-intensive, and monotonous job sectors.

Map Shows States Where People Are Most Overworked

  • Authors: Alice Gibbs
  • Link to article

The Newsweek (2024) article analyzes overwork within U.S. states regarding geography and highlights apparent differences in workload, balance of personal life, and leisure time across regions. Louisiana is labeled as the most overworked state, with an average workweek of 36.5 hours, remote work available at only 6.2%, and a high workplace death rate of 6.4 per 100,000 workers. North Dakota, widely considered the hardest working state, reports a near 98% employment rate alongside a 39.7-hour workweek, with one-third of employees not taking vacation days. Alaska has the highest average workweek at 41.6 hours per week. On average, Americans work more than 1,811 hours a year, more than most developed countries. These observations enhance research on the geography of work about state policies determining overwork, work-life balance, and employee health and welfare.

Occupation and Working Hours (ATUS 2023)

  • Authors: Bureau of Labor Statistics
  • Link to article

The 2023 American Time Use Survey by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics shows variation in working hours by occupation. Construction and extraction workers had the most extended average workday (8.69 hours), followed by production (8.43). Service work was reported to be the shortest at 7.22. Remote work patterns also differed: approximately 49% of management and finance employees worked from home, averaging 5.7 hours remotely, while only 16% of workers in service roles did. Differences by gender also persist; men work 36 minutes more than women daily. These American time-use survey trends show how a persons occupation and workplace standing elicit differing shifts in the U.S. labor market.

The reason why we chose the following variables for the simple regression model is due to the fact we believe that the variables listed above are curcial and are heavily corelated to the dependent variable (the working hour per week) in reality.

  1. Citizenship

Citizenship status is a key socioeconomic indicator that can affect the types of jobs individuals hold and the conditions under which they work. Non-citizens may face fewer job protections and more economic pressure, leading them to accept longer or less flexible work hours. Including this variable allows us to assess how legal and structural factors tied to immigration status shape labor patterns.

  1. Gender

Gender is one of the most widely studied dimensions in labor economics, and prior research consistently shows significant differences in work hours between men and women. These disparities often stem from caregiving responsibilities, occupational segregation, and societal expectations. Including gender helps us quantify how these patterns show up in weekly hours worked.

  1. Family Size

Family size is a proxy for household responsibilities and financial obligations. Larger families may push individuals to work more hours to meet household needs, although the effect could vary depending on access to childcare, social support, or cultural norms. We include this variable to test whether people with more dependents actually work longer hours.

  1. Region

Geographic region affects employment opportunities, job types, and cost of living. For example, labor markets in the South differ from those in the Northeast in terms of job structure, wages, and hours expectations. By including region, we can account for broad structural differences across U.S. labor markets that may influence how much people work.

  1. Occupation

Occupation captures the type of work someone does, which directly influences work hours. Some professions (like healthcare, construction, or management) typically demand longer schedules, overtime, or irregular hours. Including occupation helps us control for job-specific effects on work intensity and better isolate the impact of other variables like gender or citizenship.

Results

We estimated an OLS regression model to examine how weekly hours worked relate to demographic and structural factors, including citizenship, gender, family size, region, and occupation. The dependent variable is Hours_Worked_Per_Week.

  • Citizenship: The coefficient of -0.3415 (p < 0.001) suggests that citizens work about 0.34 fewer hours per week than non-citizens, holding other factors constant. This effect may reflect differences in job type, access to work, or economic necessity among non-citizens.

  • Gender: The coefficient of -3.6226 (p < 0.001) indicates that females work approximately 3.62 fewer hours per week than males, controlling for all other variables. This large and significant gap reflects persistent gender disparities in labor market participation and work hours.

  • Family Size: The coefficient of 0.0292 is not statistically significant (p = 0.125), suggesting that family size does not have a meaningful effect on hours worked when other variables are controlled for.

  • Region: Individuals in the South work significantly more hours than those in the Midwest (+1.24 hours, p < 0.001).

The Northeast and West regions have positive coefficients (+0.69 and +0.51, respectively), but these are only marginally significant (p = 0.055 and p = 0.103), suggesting weaker evidence of a regional difference.

  • Occupation: Compared to the reference group (Management, Business, Science, and Arts jobs):

Service workers work 4.11 fewer hours (p < 0.001) Sales and office workers work 2.48 fewer hours (p < 0.001) Transportation and material moving workers work 0.35 fewer hours (p < 0.01) Natural resources, construction, and maintenance workers work 1.14 fewer hours (p < 0.001)

F-statistic = 578.4 (p < 0.001): This indicates that the model is jointly significant i.e., at least one predictor has a non-zero effect on hours worked.

The models R-squared is 0.044, meaning that roughly 4.4% of the variation in weekly hours worked is explained by the included variables. While this is a modest value, the statistical significance of many coefficients shows that these factors still help explain meaningful variation in labor behavior.

Examples
--------

    The simplest way to use nbconvert is

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --to html

            Options include ['asciidoc', 'custom', 'html', 'latex', 'markdown', 'notebook', 'pdf', 'python', 'qtpdf', 'qtpng', 'rst', 'script', 'slides', 'webpdf'].

            > jupyter nbconvert --to latex mynotebook.ipynb

            Both HTML and LaTeX support multiple output templates. LaTeX includes
            'base', 'article' and 'report'.  HTML includes 'basic', 'lab' and
            'classic'. You can specify the flavor of the format used.

            > jupyter nbconvert --to html --template lab mynotebook.ipynb

            You can also pipe the output to stdout, rather than a file

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --stdout

            PDF is generated via latex

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --to pdf

            You can get (and serve) a Reveal.js-powered slideshow

            > jupyter nbconvert myslides.ipynb --to slides --post serve

            Multiple notebooks can be given at the command line in a couple of
            different ways:

            > jupyter nbconvert notebook*.ipynb
            > jupyter nbconvert notebook1.ipynb notebook2.ipynb

            or you can specify the notebooks list in a config file, containing::

                c.NbConvertApp.notebooks = ["my_notebook.ipynb"]

            > jupyter nbconvert --config mycfg.py

To see all available configurables, use `--help-all`.
